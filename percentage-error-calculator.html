Measuring Experimental Accuracy with Percentage Error

Percentage error quantifies how close your experimental measurement is to the accepted or theoretical value. It's a fundamental concept in science, engineering, and quality control. This calculator from Percentage Calculators is designed specifically for lab work and scientific analysis.

A low percentage error indicates good accuracy, while a high percentage error suggests significant deviation from the expected value and may warrant investigation of experimental procedures.

The Percentage Error Formula

Percentage Error = (|Experimental Value - Theoretical Value| ÷ |Theoretical Value|) × 100

The absolute value ensures the result is always positive (or zero), representing the magnitude of error regardless of direction.

Example Calculation

You measured the density of aluminum as 2.82 g/cm³. The accepted value is 2.70 g/cm³:

  1. Calculate absolute error: |2.82 - 2.70| = 0.12
  2. Divide by theoretical: 0.12 ÷ 2.70 = 0.0444
  3. Convert to percentage: 0.0444 × 100 = 4.44%
  4. Result: 4.44% error

Interpreting Percentage Error

Error Range Interpretation Typical Response
0-1% Excellent accuracy Results are highly reliable
1-5% Good accuracy Acceptable for most lab work
5-10% Moderate accuracy Review procedure for improvements
10-20% Poor accuracy Identify and address error sources
>20% Very poor accuracy Repeat experiment with corrections

Acceptable error depends on your field and equipment. High-precision physics experiments may require <1%, while field biology studies might accept 10%.

Types of Errors in Experiments

Systematic Errors

Consistent errors that bias all measurements in one direction. Examples: miscalibrated equipment, incorrect technique, environmental factors. These are often identifiable and correctable.

Random Errors

Unpredictable variations in measurements. Examples: reading precision limits, environmental fluctuations. Reduced by taking multiple measurements and averaging.

Human Errors

Mistakes in procedure, reading, or recording. Examples: parallax error, calculation mistakes, transcription errors. Minimized through careful technique and verification.

Common Sources of Laboratory Error

  • Equipment calibration – Scales, thermometers, and meters may drift from factory settings
  • Reading precision – Analog instruments have inherent reading uncertainty
  • Environmental conditions – Temperature, humidity, and pressure affect many measurements
  • Sample purity – Contamination affects chemical and physical properties
  • Technique variation – Timing, angles, and procedures vary between operators
  • Calculation rounding – Intermediate rounding accumulates error

Reducing Percentage Error

  1. Calibrate equipment – Verify against known standards before measuring
  2. Take multiple measurements – Average reduces random error impact
  3. Control variables – Maintain consistent conditions throughout
  4. Use appropriate precision – Match instrument precision to measurement needs
  5. Follow established protocols – Standardized procedures reduce technique variation
  6. Document everything – Track conditions and potential error sources

Related Error Calculations

Absolute Error

The simple difference between experimental and theoretical values (shown in this calculator). Useful when the magnitude of difference matters more than relative accuracy.

Relative Error

Absolute error divided by theoretical value (before multiplying by 100). Same as percentage error but expressed as a decimal.
